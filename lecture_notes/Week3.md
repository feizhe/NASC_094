## Why Are We Reading Research Papers?
Research articles are one of the main ways scientists communicate new ideas, evidence, and results.

But you do **not** need to understand every sentence the first time you read one.

Today's goal is to learn how to read a paper **strategically**, especially from a Data Science and Statistics perspective.

---
## An Example

**Esteva et al. (2017), "Dermatologist-level classification of skin cancer with deep neural networks," Nature.**


* What real-world problem are the authors trying to solve?
* What kind of data are they using?
* How are they deciding whether the model works well?
* What is the main result they want readers to believe?
* What questions or limitations should we still keep in mind?

Do not worry about understanding every technical detail right away.

Instead, focus on the big picture:
**question, data, method, result, and limitation.**

---


## Do Not Read a Paper Like a Textbook
Many students think they should start at page 1 and read every word carefully.

That is usually the wrong approach.

A better approach is:
1. get the big picture first,
2. identify the key question and result,
3. and only then go back to the technical details.

---

## First Pass: Find the Big Picture
On your first pass, focus on:
* the **title**,
* the **abstract**,
* the **introduction**,
* the main **figures or tables**,
* and the **conclusion** or discussion.

After this pass, you should be able to answer:
* What question is this paper trying to answer?
* Why does the question matter?
* What kind of data did they use?
* What is the main claim?

---

## Data Science and Statistics Lens
When reading a paper in Data Science or Statistics, look for these ingredients:
* **Research question:** what are they trying to predict, estimate, compare, or explain?
* **Data source:** where did the data come from?
* **Population or sample:** who or what does the dataset represent?
* **Variables:** what are the inputs and outputs?
* **Method:** regression, experiment, causal design, machine learning model, or something else?
* **Evaluation:** how do they decide whether the method worked?
* **Uncertainty:** do they report confidence intervals, error rates, or other limits?
* **Generalizability:** will this still work on other data or in other settings?

---

## What Can You Ignore on the First Pass?
It is okay to skip some things at first:
* dense equations,
* long literature review details,
* every citation,
* and technical implementation details that are not needed for the main idea.

You are not being lazy.

You are deciding what matters **first**.

Once you understand the main claim, the harder details become easier to place.

---



## Questions to Ask While Reading
Even if the paper sounds impressive, ask critical questions:
* Is the dataset representative of the real world?
* How were the labels created, and could they contain errors?
* Are the evaluation metrics appropriate for the problem?
* Did the authors compare against strong baselines?
* Could bias in the data affect performance?
* Would the method still work in a different hospital, city, or population?

This is what it means to read with **curiosity** and **skepticism**.

---

## Common Mistakes When Reading Papers
Some common mistakes are:
* trying to understand every line before understanding the big idea,
* assuming a paper is correct because it is published,
* ignoring the data collection process,
* focusing only on the method and not the limitations,
* and confusing a strong result on one dataset with universal truth.

Good readers are active readers.

---

## Wrap-Up
Main takeaway:

**You do not need to understand everything in a research paper to learn from it.**

A strong first reading should help you identify:
* the question,
* the data,
* the method,
* the main result,
* and the limitations.
