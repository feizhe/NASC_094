## What Kind of Problems Do I Work On?
My research sits at the intersection of:
* **statistics,**
* **machine learning and AI,**
* **biomedical and health data,**
* and **scientific collaboration.**

I work on questions such as:
* How can we make reliable conclusions from high-dimensional data?
* How do we measure uncertainty in modern prediction models?
* How can image data help detect disease?
* How can data improve public health and clinical care?

---

## Research Theme 1 - Statistical Inference
Some of my work asks a basic but powerful question:

**If a model gives an answer, how much should we trust it?**

Examples from my publications:
* high-dimensional linear models,
* generalized linear models,
* censored quantile regression,
* and prediction intervals for lasso and neural networks.

Recent examples include work published in **JASA** and **JMLR**, along with newer work on **U-learning for prediction inference**.

This is the kind of research that builds the mathematical foundation behind data science.

---

## Research Theme 2 - Learning from Images
Another part of my work uses **deep learning with image data**, especially in glaucoma research.

Recent examples include work on:
* grading fundus images,
* identifying the optic disc rim,
* smoothing visual field data,
* and newer projects on feature selection and vision transformers.

These projects connect to recent papers in **Ophthalmology Science**, **JAMA Ophthalmology**, and current submitted work on image-based glaucoma detection.

This area shows that data science is not only about spreadsheets.  
It can also help doctors detect disease earlier and more accurately.

## Research Theme 3 - Data for Health & Society
I have also worked on collaborative projects involving:
* opioid use disorder and rural health,
* social determinants of health,
* pregnancy and metabolomics,
* COVID-19 related health analytics,
* and large language models for medical data extraction.

These collaborations led to papers in journals such as **IEEE Transactions on Big Data**, **Journal of Addiction Medicine**, **NEJM AI**, and other health science venues.

One thing I learned: **important research often happens in teams**.  
Many meaningful questions come from medicine, public health, and real community needs.

## A Surprising Example - Aging Across Mammals
Some of the most unexpected projects in my career involved **DNA methylation and aging**.

These collaborations led to papers on:
* beluga whales,
* dolphins,
* humpback whales,
* sea lions and seals,
* mammalian aging clocks,
* and lifespan-related biological traits.

Some of this work appeared in **Science**, **Nature Aging**, **Nature Communications**, and **Science Advances**.

This is a reminder that statistics can travel anywhere, from hospitals to ocean science.

## What Research Really Feels Like
Research is usually:
* slower than expected,
* messier than expected,
* and more collaborative than expected.

A paper may begin with:
* a simple question,
* an imperfect dataset,
* many failed ideas,
* and lots of revision.

That is normal. Progress in science is often built from persistence, not instant success.

Advice I want you to remember:
* **Build strong fundamentals.** Math, coding, writing, and communication all matter.
* **Do not wait to be “ready.”** Start small and learn by doing.
* **Ask questions early.** Curiosity is more valuable than pretending to know everything.
* **Find mentors and collaborators.** Most opportunities come through people.
* **Stay open-minded.** Your future interests may be very different from what you expect today.

Your first goal is not to have a perfect career plan.  
Your first goal is to keep building capacity.
